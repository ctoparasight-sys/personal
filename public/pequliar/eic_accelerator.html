Section 2

Opportunity / Problem

4,997 / 5,000 characters

300 million people. Fewer than 100 treated. In 20 years.

Approximately 300 million people worldwide live with rare genetic diseases — mutations that drive devastating conditions, many without a name in medical literature, let alone a treatment. 70% have pediatric onset, and an estimated 30% of children with rare diseases do not survive past their fifth birthday (Nguengang Wakap et al., Eur J Hum Genet 2020;28:165-173; Lancet Glob Health 2024;12:e341). Their families search desperately for any information, any pathway, any fighting chance. Most find nothing.

Antisense oligonucleotides (ASOs) are among the most versatile tools for correcting genetic defects. Unlike RNAi (which can only silence genes) or gene editing and mRNA therapies (which require viral vectors and complex delivery), ASOs are synthetic DNA molecules that can silence, skip, switch, block, or upregulate gene expression through six distinct mechanisms. They are pharmacologically simple, clinically validated, and increasingly well-understood. More than a dozen ASO drugs have received regulatory approval from the FDA and EMA (Al Musaimi et al., Pharmaceuticals 2025;18:291), treating conditions from spinal muscular atrophy to hereditary transthyretin amyloidosis to Duchenne muscular dystrophy.

Yet the knowledge to design ASOs for individual patients remains locked behind pharmaceutical R&D infrastructure costing tens of millions of euros and 5-10 years per program. n-Lorem Foundation, founded by Ionis pioneer Stanley Crooke, provides personalised ASO medicines free of charge for life to patients with nano-rare mutations affecting 1-30 individuals worldwide — an extraordinary charitable effort that has treated 50 patients to date, with 240 more accepted and waiting (n-Lorem Foundation, 21 April 2026). But n-Lorem's deliberate focus on the rarest mutations, and its requirement for US-based patients at tertiary care centres, means the vast majority of the 300 million rare disease patients worldwide — including those whose mutations affect hundreds or thousands — have no pathway to access ASO design expertise.

The science itself works. Milasen, an ASO designed for a single child with CLN7 Batten disease, demonstrated that individualised ASO therapy can be conceived, designed, and administered within months (Kim et al., N Engl J Med 2019;381:1644-52). The regulatory framework exists: the FDA has issued three dedicated draft guidances for IND submissions for individualised ASO drug products (FDA-2020-D-2199; FDA-2021-D-1140; FDA-2021-D-1139). The failure is one of access to knowledge and infrastructure.

Physicians treating rare disease patients face an impossible gap. They are clinicians, not ASO designers. They lack the computational tools, thermodynamic models, and oligonucleotide chemistry expertise required to identify optimal target sites, evaluate binding energetics, screen for off-target effects, assign chemical modifications, and assess manufacturability. This expertise exists in a handful of pharmaceutical companies and academic laboratories. It is not accessible to the 300 million patients who need it.

The market opportunity reflects the economics of information versus drug development. Developing an ASO as a pharmaceutical product costs EUR 30-50 million and requires enough patients to justify commercial return — which most rare diseases do not have. This condemns the majority of patients to permanent therapeutic neglect. The alternative is not cheaper drugs but a fundamentally different model: providing scientific design and analysis as information, at a price accessible to families.

At a deliberately non-prohibitive price of EUR 370 per design — comparable to a specialist consultation — even conservative adoption of 1 in 1,000 affected individuals represents a EUR 111 million market. At 1% adoption, the opportunity exceeds EUR 1 billion. This pricing is designed to be affordable enough that a parent seeking any chance for their child can access cutting-edge ASO design science, while sustaining a business that serves patients at scale.

The opportunity is not to develop another ASO drug. It is to democratise the knowledge that makes ASO therapy design possible — and make it accessible to every rare disease family in the world.

Section 3

Company

4,993 / 5,000 characters

Pequliar was founded in 2026 by Ido Bachelet, Ph.D., to solve a problem he has spent 17 years preparing to address: making antisense oligonucleotide design accessible to every rare disease patient in the world, not just the handful served by pharmaceutical R&D.

The Founder

Bachelet holds a Ph.D. in Pharmacology and Experimental Therapeutics Design from the Hebrew University of Jerusalem and a B.Sc.Med. from Hadassah Medical School. He trained at MIT (Sasisekharan lab) and Harvard Medical School / Wyss Institute (George Church lab), where he pioneered programmable DNA nanostructures for therapeutic applications, publishing in Science (2012) and Nature Nanotechnology (2014). He holds 30+ patents, including patents on mRNA target identification and therapeutic oligonucleotide design — the core science underlying Pequliar. Over the past 13 years he has co-founded five companies that collectively raised over EUR 310 million, including 1E Therapeutics (EUR 110M seed round, 2021 — the largest biotech seed in Israeli history), the first XNAzyme company, developing a novel class of catalytic oligonucleotides for RNA silencing. Bachelet brings a rare combination of deep nucleic acid science, computational biology expertise, and proven entrepreneurial execution.

The Product

Pequliar is an AI-powered platform (pequliar.ai) that performs end-to-end ASO design for individual patients. A patient or physician describes their genetic condition — by uploading medical records, genetic test results, or simply describing the mutation in natural language. The platform's AI agent identifies the affected gene, determines the optimal ASO mechanism (from six supported: gene-silencing, exon-skipping, splice-switching, pseudoexon-blocking, upregulation, and heteroplasmy-shifting), and executes a full computational design pipeline. Pequliar utilises state-of-the-art AI and computational tools for RNA analysis that encompass the most current knowledge of how RNA folds, breathes, resists correction, and interacts with therapeutic oligonucleotides. Critically, each candidate is evaluated against 51 heuristic quality checks built on 17 years of data and models — including unique datasets from atomic force microscopy and single-molecule spectroscopy — covering domains from predicted toxicity profiles to manufacturing feasibility to mechanism-specific efficacy. The output is a ranked set of optimised ASO candidates with full chemistry assignment, quality annotations, and a downloadable synthesis-ready report — delivered in minutes, not months.

Current Status (TRL 6-7)

The platform is live in production. The computational pipeline has been validated against approved ASO drugs, recovering 47% of known therapeutics in the top-10 ranked candidates — compared to 20% for existing academic tools. A 100-patient batch simulation achieved a 93.4% completion rate with zero pipeline failures. A physician pilot study is underway across three leading medical centres in Israel (Schneider Children's Hospital, Sheba Medical Center, Meir Hospital), with 20-30 patients per centre given free access. Early pilot cases include a heteroplasmy-shifting ASO for a child with the m.A3243G mutation driving MELAS syndrome, and a gene-silencing ASO targeting an Asp437 deletion in COMP causing pseudoachondroplasia — a case the treating physicians had considered impossible to design. The platform supports all six ASO mechanisms end-to-end, including heteroplasmy-shifting for mitochondrial mutations — a mechanism no other tool offers.

Business Model

EUR 370 per design, paid by the patient. No subscription, no recurring fees. One mutation, one design, one payment. Beyond direct patient access, Pequliar pursues two additional market strategies: first, licensing the platform as a design engine to pharmaceutical companies developing rare disease therapies (initial discussions with Chiesi, Italy); and second, partnerships with AI companies increasingly investing in AI for health and medicine.

Vision and Use of Funds

The EIC grant will fund: founder salary, a full-stack engineer (50% position, 12 months), external consultants (legal, regulatory, clinical), targeted validation studies on pilot cases, IP protection (patent filings covering the multi-mechanism design pipeline and heuristic scoring system), and marketing and patient outreach. Within 24 months, Pequliar aims to reach 100,000 patients worldwide.

Why Private Investment Is Insufficient at This Stage

1. A new category between biotech VC and SaaS VC

Pequliar's EUR 370 per-design model resembles expert consulting — a well-understood business. However, it is B2C (patients, not enterprises), transactional (not subscription), and in a domain (rare disease) where biotech VCs expect drug candidates and pipeline valuations, while software VCs expect enterprise contracts with annual recurring revenue. Pequliar fits neither framework cleanly. The pharma licensing channel would satisfy biotech investors, and patient volume would satisfy SaaS investors — but at pre-revenue stage, neither story is yet proven. Non-dilutive EIC funding bridges this gap without forcing premature positioning into either category.

2. Premature equity would distort the mission

Having raised EUR 310M+ across five previous ventures, Bachelet is acutely aware of the distortions that premature equity financing creates — particularly in a company whose mission is to serve patients affordably. VC investment at this stage would create pressure to increase pricing, restrict access, or pivot toward pharmaceutical licensing as the primary business — undermining the core patient-access mission. The EIC grant provides non-dilutive capital that preserves the company's ability to keep prices accessible while building toward scale. Once patient volume demonstrates product-market fit, private investment can be raised on the company's own terms.

3. EIC is uniquely aligned

The EIC Accelerator is designed precisely for deep-tech companies at TRL 6-7 that need to bridge from validated technology to market scale. Pequliar's platform is built, validated, and in physician pilot — but requires investment in marketing, patient outreach, IP protection, and clinical validation to reach the scale where revenue sustains operations. This is exactly the gap non-dilutive EIC funding is designed to fill, without the mission-distorting pressure of equity investors optimising for short-term returns.

7Case Studies — Physician Pilot

Already designing what physicians thought impossible

Pilot study underway across three leading Israeli medical centres (Schneider Children's Hospital, Sheba Medical Center, Meir Hospital). 20-30 patients per centre, free access.

Case 1: MELAS — m.A3243G in MT-TL1 (tRNA-Leu)

The most prevalent mutation driving MELAS syndrome. Every conventional ASO approach fails: the target is a noncoding RNA (tRNA-Leu), so it cannot be silenced. Mitochondrial DNA has no exons or introns, ruling out exon-skipping. The tRNA's rigid three-dimensional structure makes it inaccessible to DNAzymes. Pequliar designed a heteroplasmy-shifting ASO — a mechanism class no other tool supports — that selectively binds the mutant mitochondrial DNA to shift the heteroplasmic ratio in favour of wild-type copies.

Case 2: Pseudoachondroplasia — Asp437 deletion in COMP

A girl with a dominant GAC deletion in a multiple GAC repeat region of COMP (wild-type: 5×GAC, mutant: 4×GAC). The sequences flanking the deletion are practically identical, making allele-selective targeting seemingly impossible. Pequliar analysed base modification catalogues across all oligonucleotide synthesis vendors and designed a region of targeted mismatches using non-hydrogen-bonding base analogues — generating an ASO that preferentially binds mutant over wild-type at approximately 50% occupancy.

These are real children. These are the cases the platform was built for.

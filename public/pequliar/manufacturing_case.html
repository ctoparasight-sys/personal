Manufacturing Standards for Individualized ASO Therapies

The Case for Proportionate Manufacturing Standards in Individualized Antisense Oligonucleotide Therapies

Why rigorous quality control, not facility certification, should define the manufacturing standard for single-patient oligonucleotide products.

Executive Summary

Core Thesis

The argument in three sentences

Current regulatory frameworks assume that clinical-grade drugs require current Good Manufacturing Practice (cGMP) compliance under 21 CFR Parts 210–211. For individualized antisense oligonucleotide (ASO) therapies — single-patient, single-batch products designed for rare or ultra-rare mutations — this assumption imposes a 10–20× cost multiplier and 6–12 month delays that warrant re-examination.

This paper presents the case that research-grade oligonucleotide synthesis with appropriate quality control (QC) testing produces a product that is chemically identical, equally safe, and fully characterized — and that the regulatory framework already contains the exemptions to support this approach.

GMP manufacturing adds process documentation and facility certification. It does not add a single atom to the molecule, improve its purity, or increase its safety. For a fully characterizable synthetic product like an oligonucleotide, quality can be tested into the product — which transcends the foundational assumption on which cGMP was built.

Section I

The Foundational Assumption of GMP — And Why Oligonucleotides Transcend It

The premise that quality cannot be tested in does not apply to synthetic oligonucleotides

A. Why GMP Exists

The cGMP framework (21 CFR Parts 210–211, originally codified 1978) was designed around a principle articulated by the FDA:

“Quality cannot be tested into a product; it must be built in by design.”

This principle is valid for complex biological products (vaccines, blood products), fermentation-derived molecules, and formulated dosage forms where:

  • The manufacturing process introduces variability that testing cannot fully detect
  • Impurities may be process-dependent and not predictable from the final product alone
  • Batch-to-batch consistency requires controlled conditions because the product cannot be fully characterized

B. Oligonucleotides Are Fully Characterizable

Antisense oligonucleotides transcend this foundational assumption. An ASO is a short (typically 15–25 nucleotide), chemically defined synthetic molecule produced by solid-phase phosphoramidite synthesis — an automated, deterministic, stepwise chemical process invented in 1981 and refined over 45 years. The product is fully characterizable by analytical methods:

QC TestWhat It ConfirmsSensitivity
Mass spectrometry (ESI-MS)Molecular identity — correct sequence and modificationsSingle-dalton resolution; detects any sequence error
HPLCPurity — percentage of full-length productDetects impurities at <0.1%
Capillary gel electrophoresisLength distribution — absence of truncated sequencesSingle-nucleotide resolution
Endotoxin (USP ⟨85⟩)Pyrogen-free — safe for injection<0.5 EU/mL threshold
Sterility (USP ⟨71⟩)No microbial contamination14-day culture (pharmacopeial)
Residual solventsAbsence of process chemicalsppm-level detection (GC-MS)

If two oligonucleotides — one manufactured under cGMP and one under research-grade conditions — pass the same panel of QC tests, they are analytically indistinguishable. No test exists that can differentiate them. The GMP label does not confer a physical, chemical, or biological property that QC testing cannot verify.

C. The Analogy That Makes This Obvious

PCR primers are oligonucleotides synthesized by the identical phosphoramidite chemistry, on the same or similar synthesizers, using the same reagents. PCR primers are used routinely in clinical diagnostics — including FDA-cleared in vitro diagnostic tests — without GMP manufacturing. The chemistry is the same. The QC is the same. The distinction is regulatory classification, not molecular reality.

C. What GMP Adds That QC Does Not

The true difference between GMP and research-grade manufacturing is documentation infrastructure:

  • Validated SOPs with change control procedures
  • Environmental monitoring programs (viable and non-viable particle counts)
  • Formal deviation investigation and CAPA processes
  • Equipment qualification protocols (IQ/OQ/PQ)
  • Validated cleaning procedures between batches
  • Formal supplier qualification programs
  • Quality management system (QMS) with internal audits

These are valuable for a facility producing thousands of batches per year where process drift could affect quality across many patients. For a single-patient product verified by analytical QC, they add cost and time without adding safety.

Cost Impact

GMP synthesis facility$350K–$1.2M equipment + $200K–$500K/yr compliance
Research-grade synthesizer$30K–$120K
Cost multiplier10–20×

The cost multiplier is driven almost entirely by infrastructure amortization, not by the chemistry.

Section III

The FDA Already Provides the Exemptions

Federal regulation, not guidance — Phase 1 drugs are exempt from Part 211

A. 21 CFR 210.2(c) — The Phase 1 Blanket Exemption

“The regulations set forth in this part and in parts 211 through 226 of this chapter… shall not apply to any Phase 1 drug.”

— 21 CFR 210.2(c)

This is not a guidance document or a recommendation. It is federal regulation. Phase 1 investigational drugs are explicitly exempt from all of Part 211 (cGMP). An individualized ASO under an IND is, by definition, a Phase 1 drug.

B. FDA Phase 1 CGMP Guidance (July 2008)

The FDA’s guidance “CGMP for Phase 1 Investigational Drugs” replaces the full Part 211 requirements with eight minimum quality elements:

  1. Personnel qualifications and training
  2. Facility and equipment suitable for intended purpose
  3. Control of components (raw materials)
  4. Production and process controls
  5. Laboratory controls (QC testing)
  6. Packaging and labeling
  7. Documentation and records
  8. Change control procedures (basic)

These eight elements are routinely met by competent research-grade synthesis laboratories. The guidance explicitly states that Phase 1 manufacturing does not require:

  • Validated manufacturing processes
  • Full environmental monitoring programs
  • Formal equipment qualification (IQ/OQ/PQ)
  • Validated analytical methods (fit-for-purpose is sufficient)
  • Full stability programs

C. FDA Individualized ASO CMC Guidance (2021)

The FDA issued four draft guidance documents in 2021 specifically for individualized ASO IND applications. The Chemistry, Manufacturing, and Controls (CMC) guidance:

  • Confirms that 21 CFR 210.2(c) applies — the initial batch is exempt from Part 211
  • Permits the same batch for nonclinical studies and initial clinical dosing — reducing manufacturing runs
  • Accepts a platform-based CMC approach — individual products reference the platform without full per-product CMC
  • Requires release testing (identity, purity, sterility, endotoxin) but does not mandate GMP facility certification for the initial batch

D. 503A Compounding Pharmacy Precedent

Under Section 503A of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act, compounding pharmacies produce millions of sterile injectable products annually without cGMP compliance. They operate under:

  • USP ⟨797⟩ sterile compounding standards
  • State pharmacy board oversight
  • No FDA pre-approval of formulations
  • No Part 211 compliance

Compounded sterile products include intrathecal injections, IV infusions, and ophthalmic preparations — routes of administration identical to those used for ASO delivery.

Limitation: 503A requires that the drug substance be a component of an FDA-approved drug or listed in the USP/NF. Novel ASO sequences would not qualify under current 503A provisions. However, the precedent demonstrates that the FDA accepts non-GMP manufacturing of injectable products when appropriate quality standards are maintained.

E. CBER Cell and Gene Therapy Guidance (January 2026)

The FDA’s Center for Biologics Evaluation and Research (CBER) issued updated guidance in January 2026 for cell and gene therapy products stating that compliance with 21 CFR Part 211 is not expected before Phase 2/3 clinical trials. While this applies to biologics, it establishes a current regulatory precedent that investigational products — including those administered to patients — do not require full cGMP compliance.

Section IV

Clinical Precedents: Non-GMP Manufacturing That Worked

Equivalent outcomes at a fraction of the cost

A. Academic CAR-T Cell Therapy

Commercial CAR-T products (Kymriah, Yescarta) cost $373K–$475K per treatment, manufactured in GMP facilities. Academic medical centers have demonstrated equivalent clinical outcomes at dramatically lower cost:

SiteManufacturingCost per Patient
Commercial (Novartis/Gilead)Full GMP$373K–$475K
Sheba Medical Center (Israel)Academic~$47K
University of PennsylvaniaPhase 1 exemptionResearch cost
India (multiple centers)Non-commercial$20K–$47K

The safety record of academic CAR-T is comparable to commercial products. The ~10× cost difference is driven by GMP facility costs and commercial overhead, not by product quality.

B. Milasen (2018)

Milasen, the first individualized ASO for clinical use (for Mila Makovec, CLN7 Batten disease), was manufactured by TriLink BioTechnologies. While TriLink had GMP capability, the regulatory pathway under which milasen was administered did not require it. The total development timeline was ~10 months — remarkably fast, but still too slow for patients in acute decline.

C. n-Lorem Foundation

The n-Lorem Foundation, founded by Stanley Crooke (former CEO of Ionis Pharmaceuticals), has treated 50+ patients with individualized ASOs. Dr. Crooke has stated publicly:

“Manufacturing the oligo itself is the cheap part.”

n-Lorem uses Ionis’s existing GMP infrastructure because it is available, not because it is required. The per-patient cost of ~$700K–$1.5M is driven by nonclinical studies, regulatory overhead, and clinical management — not by synthesis cost.

Section V

The Scientific Argument

Why GMP cannot add value to a fully characterized product

A. The Deterministic Nature of Phosphoramidite Synthesis

Oligonucleotide synthesis is a deterministic, stepwise chemical reaction:

  1. Each coupling cycle adds exactly one nucleotide to the growing chain
  2. Coupling efficiency is >99% per step (measured in real-time by trityl monitoring)
  3. The synthesizer is automated — human variability is minimal
  4. Failed couplings produce truncated sequences that are removed by purification
  5. The final product is a single, defined chemical entity

This is fundamentally different from biological manufacturing (cell culture, fermentation) where the process is stochastic, post-translational modifications are unpredictable, and the product is too complex to fully characterize.

For biologics, GMP process controls are essential because you cannot test everything. For oligonucleotides, you can test everything — and the QC panel does exactly that.

B. Chemical Modifications Do Not Change This Analysis

Modern therapeutic ASOs use chemical modifications (2′-O-methoxyethyl, phosphorothioate backbone, locked nucleic acids, etc.) synthesized using the same phosphoramidite approach with modified amidite reagents. These modifications:

  • Are commercially available as pre-synthesized phosphoramidites
  • Couple with the same or similar efficiency as natural nucleotides
  • Are detected by the same QC methods (ESI-MS resolves the mass difference)
  • Do not introduce process variability beyond what is captured by QC testing

C. The “Indistinguishable Tubes” Argument

If you placed two oligonucleotides — one GMP, one research-grade — side by side after identical purification and QC:

Same mass spectrum→same molecular identity
Same HPLC purity→same purity profile
Same CGE pattern→same length distribution
Same sterility result→same sterility
Same endotoxin level→same pyrogenicity

No analytical method exists to distinguish them. The GMP label represents a claim about the process, not about the product. For a fully characterizable molecule, the product speaks for itself.

C. Access

At $300K–$500K for manufacturing alone (before nonclinical studies, clinical management, and regulatory fees), individualized ASO therapy is accessible only to patients in wealthy countries with philanthropic support (n-Lorem) or extraordinary fundraising ability.

Reducing manufacturing costs to $15K–$40K would make individualized ASO therapy accessible to a vastly larger patient population — including in middle-income countries where the disease burden of rare genetic conditions is substantial but resources are limited.

Section IX

Frequently Raised Concerns

Common questions and our perspective

“GMP is the gold standard for patient safety”

GMP is a gold standard for process consistency in mass manufacturing. For single-patient products that are fully characterized by analytical testing, patient safety is ensured by the QC results, not by the process documentation. The question is not whether GMP is good — it is whether GMP adds safety beyond what QC testing provides for a fully characterizable product. For oligonucleotides, the answer is no.

“Research-grade synthesis may have impurities that GMP would prevent”

All impurities in oligonucleotide synthesis are detectable by the QC panel. Truncated sequences (the primary impurity) are detected by HPLC and CGE with single-nucleotide resolution. Depurination products are detected by ESI-MS. Process-related impurities (residual solvents, deprotection reagents) are detected by GC-MS. There are no “hidden” impurities that GMP process controls would prevent but QC testing would miss.

“What about sterility assurance?”

Sterility is ensured by terminal 0.22 µm filtration and confirmed by USP ⟨71⟩ testing. This is the same approach used in 503A compounding pharmacies for sterile injectable products. The sterility assurance level is a function of the filtration and testing protocol, not the GMP status of the facility.

“Regulatory agencies will not accept non-GMP products”

The FDA’s own regulations (21 CFR 210.2(c)) exempt Phase 1 drugs from Part 211. The FDA’s own guidance documents (2008 Phase 1 CGMP, 2021 Individualized ASO CMC) confirm this exemption. Multiple international agencies (TGA, Israel MOH, Japan) already permit non-GMP investigational products. The regulatory framework supports this approach — the barrier is convention, not law.

“n-Lorem uses GMP manufacturing”

n-Lorem uses Ionis’s existing GMP infrastructure because it is available, not because it is required. Dr. Crooke has stated that manufacturing is “the cheap part” — the cost driver is nonclinical studies and regulatory overhead, not synthesis. Even within the GMP framework, the per-patient cost exceeds $700K. Reducing manufacturing costs alone will not solve the access problem — the entire regulatory paradigm must be proportionate to the clinical context.

Section X

Conclusion

A framework whose time has come

Applying full cGMP manufacturing to individualized ASOs is a regulatory convention that deserves re-examination:

  1. Rests on an assumption that does not apply — that quality cannot be tested into an oligonucleotide product (it can, and the QC panel proves it)
  2. Is already exempted by the FDA’s own regulations — 21 CFR 210.2(c) blanket-exempts Phase 1 drugs from Part 211
  3. Is inconsistent with existing practice — 503A compounding pharmacies produce sterile injectables without cGMP; academic CAR-T programs achieve comparable outcomes at a fraction of commercial GMP cost
  4. Imposes disproportionate costs — 10–20× cost multiplier for documentation infrastructure that does not improve the molecule
  5. Creates barriers to access — months of delay for patients with progressive, fatal diseases

The path forward is a testing-based quality framework that mandates rigorous analytical QC (identity, purity, sterility, endotoxin) while eliminating process documentation requirements designed for mass manufacturing. This approach preserves patient safety, is supported by the science of oligonucleotide chemistry, is consistent with existing regulatory exemptions, and could reduce the cost of individualized ASO manufacturing from $300K–$500K to under $40K — making personalized antisense medicine accessible to patients worldwide.
